Yes — this is the right move, and most of it is sound. The reframing from "identify v" to "certify functional proximity to every clean candidate" is exactly how you should handle an unidentifiable nuisance parameter, and the load-bearing trick — clamping b to b^eff via the domain bound μ_j, which converts infinite parameter ambiguity into finite *behavioral* ambiguity — is genuinely the key insight. Steps 3–5 check out: the fit-preservation argument is correct, the Lipschitz-plus-triangle-inequality derivation is valid as far as it goes, the worked example's numbers are right, and showing the bound is *achieved* at x = 10 (not just asserted) is the kind of tightness check most write-ups skip. The R_j monitor in Step 6 is the most practically useful artifact here. That said, there is one gap that affects validity, not just tightness, and it needs to move from a literature footnote to a stated hypothesis.

**1. The certificate is only valid under an unstated identifiability assumption — and it can be *false* without it.** Step 4 claims "exercised units contribute zero (their bias is exact)," but exercised biases are not generally pinned by D. Concretely: A = [1, −1], B = [1; 1]ᵀ, D = {(0, 0.5)}, X = [−10, 10]. Every b = (c + 0.5, c) with c ≥ 0 fits D exactly, both units fire on the data point, yet the candidates are genuinely different functions — at x = −5, the c = 0 model outputs 0 and the c = 9 model outputs 0.5. Your procedure would "recover" one candidate, assign both units width zero, and print a small ε\* that the true clean model violates. This is the cancellation-direction problem: whenever the A-columns of co-active units are linearly dependent, biases can slide along null directions of the active submatrix. Step 7 acknowledges this but frames it as the attacker's trick and Fine-Pruning's blind spot; it's actually a hypothesis your Steps 1–4 silently require. State it: *for each data point's activation pattern, the active columns of A are linearly independent, and the pattern assignment consistent with D is unique up to function equivalence.* (Your own running example quietly relies on the last clause — the swapped branch (b₂ = −1, b₁ ≤ −5) is also feasible and only harmless because the symmetry makes it function-identical.) The honest general statement is that ε\* must bound the function-space diameter of the *joint* feasible set; the per-unit interval certificate is the special case where that set factorizes.

**2. Exact interpolation is load-bearing and won't survive contact with anything real.** If the fit is only ‖f(xᵢ) − yᵢ‖ ≤ δ, exercised biases become intervals of width O(δ) too, and dormancy becomes uncertain (a unit could fire slightly and hide inside the tolerance). The machinery survives — everything becomes slabs instead of points — but you should derive the δ-version before trusting any empirical instantiation, since for the LLM motivation δ = 0 never holds.

**3. ε\* has known slack you should name.** The triangle inequality sums per-unit worst cases that are attained at *different* x's and ignores sign cancellation across A's columns. It's tight in your example only because there's a single dormant unit. The exact worst-case gap over the interval box is computable as a MILP for ReLU nets — worth offering as an optional tightening, with ε\* as the cheap sound relaxation.

**4. Say out loud that the pipeline never queries the black box.** The repair is rebuilt entirely from (A, B, D, X); b' is discarded. That's a feature under your threat model — the certificate holds against *unbounded* v, which is rare and worth advertising — but it means you throw away information whenever v is actually small. Under a bounded adversary ‖v‖∞ ≤ ρ, clipping the observed b'_j into [μ_j, τ_j] beats the midpoint whenever ρ < w_j/2. Framing midpoint-vs-clip-vs-kill (μ_j) as three points on an explicit loss trade-off would sharpen Step 2, which currently gestures at this only for the kill option.

**5. On the motivating question:** the bias-only threat model is a better match to LLM steering than it might look — residual-stream steering vectors *are* additive activation offsets. The real bottleneck when scaling up isn't the algebra, it's characterizing X: the reachable set at layer ℓ is not a box, bound propagation will give loose μ_j's, and loose μ inflates every w_j and hence ε\*. That, plus point 2, is where this framework will strain first.

Verdict: good idea, correct core, and honest by the standards of the genre — but as written the certificate overclaims because Issue 1 is a validity condition, not a limitation footnote. Fix that and you have a defensible skeleton.
