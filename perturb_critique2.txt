Yes — the pivot from "recover v" to "certify the unidentifiable residue is harmless on-distribution" is the right move, and it's the modern one (this is conformal-prediction machinery applied per unit). I checked the math line by line; Steps 2–4 are sound as written, and the arithmetic checks out (ln(10⁵)/10⁴ ≈ 0.115%, the ~870× shrinkage claim is fair). Two details deserve explicit credit: union-bounding over all h units rather than just the observed-dormant ones is what makes the D-dependent selection of "dormant" legitimate, and the Step 5/6 observation that stealth squeezes attacker power into the tails — deriving trigger-based backdoors from first principles — is genuinely the best paragraph in the document.

That said, three things are load-bearing and either unstated or understated. In order of severity:

**1. Exchangeability requires the audit set to be independent of B. This can silently kill the whole certificate.** Step 2 treats s₁,…,s_N, s as N+1 i.i.d. draws. That's only true if the direction B_j was fixed *before* D was drawn. In your original setup, D is the dataset the model fits exactly — i.e., plausibly the training set — and then B_j is a function of x₁,…,x_N, so (s₁,…,s_N, s) is not exchangeable. Concretely: a row direction correlated with the sample can make max_i s_i atypically small, and the record probability can be Θ(1) instead of 1/(N+1). Nothing in the proof survives this. The fix is cheap but must be stated: the audit set must be fresh i.i.d. draws collected after the model is fixed — e.g., logged pre-injection traffic, with labels defined as y_i := f_clean(x_i), which also gives you exact fit for free. If instead the toy stipulates B is fixed independently of D by fiat, fine, but say so; when you port this to real steering-vector detection the distinction is fatal.

**2. Where does a finite w_j come from?** Dormancy alone gives half-infinite feasible intervals (−∞, τ_j], under which "midpoint" and w_j/2 are undefined and both certificates are vacuous. Part I is evidently supplying a lower edge — presumably an attack budget ‖v‖_∞ ≤ V or a bias prior. Whatever it is, surface it: the probability half of the bound is distribution-free, but the *magnitude* half is not free — the cap w_j is exactly what spares you tail-moment assumptions on P. And Step 5's "attacker damage ≤ 2ε*·ln(h/δ)/N" only binds attackers who respect that budget.

**3. Per-unit identifiability = full column rank A, and your example numbers contradict it.** Determining dormancy (h_ij = 0 ∀i) and exactly repairing active units requires recovering hidden activations from outputs, i.e., k ≥ h. With h = 1000 hidden units and any realistic output dimension, you can't run the interval decomposition at all. Step 6 acknowledges "per-unit blindness" as an attack vector, but it equally breaks the *defense's* premise — promote it from limitation to standing assumption, or the concrete numbers advertise a regime where the method doesn't apply as stated.

Smaller notes, briefly: the bound controls expected magnitude, not frequency — P(any deviation) ≤ Σq_j can be ~h/N ≈ 10% at your numbers, so phrase the claim as "deviations are tiny on average," not "outputs are usually identical." In pure expectation over D you get ε*/(N+1) with no log factor; the ln(h/δ) is purely the price of the high-probability form, worth one sentence. Ties/atoms: all inequalities survive, the equality remarks need continuity of the projection distribution. Exact refinement available: q_j ~ Beta(1, N) for continuous P, and a weighted union bound (δ_j ∝ ‖A_{:,j}‖w_j) tightens the sum slightly — neither is necessary. And Step 6's partition is really "adversarial vs *i.i.d.* benign": benign drift also violates exchangeability and is covered by neither certificate.

One upgrade worth adding, since it's nearly free: R_j membership is *observable at inference time* (B and τ_j are known). So the defender doesn't just get a statistical bound — they get a runtime monitor: if a fresh input breaks no dormant unit's record, the repaired output equals the clean output *exactly, provably, for that input*; if it does break one, flag or abstain, with guaranteed false-flag rate ≤ h/(N+1) in expectation. That converts "impotent on average" into "either exact or flagged," which is a much stronger operational story.

Bottom line: good idea, correct core proof, and the honest scoping in Step 6 is a strength rather than a weakness. But fix #1 before anything else — as currently set up (D = the exact-fit dataset), the exchangeability step is not valid, and it's the foundation everything else stands on.
