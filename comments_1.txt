The skeleton is sound and one of your observations is sharp, but there are three conflations buried in the prose that have to be fixed before this is a construction rather than a slogan, and there's an existing literature you haven't named that threatens the novelty. Let me be specific.

**What's right.** The Term A / Term B split is exactly Malach et al.'s Theorem 2 decomposition (their $\tilde\theta$ trajectory is your Term B, the zero-label gradient), so you're standing on real ground. And the genuinely good noticing is this: their strong separation (leaky parities) exploits *only* the first moment $\mathbb{E}[x]=\pm\alpha$. Nobody states that explicitly in the paper, and asking whether GD's marginal advantage survives when the signal is pushed to moment-blind orders is a legitimate, non-obvious question. Keep that. It's the core of whatever this becomes.

**Conflation 1 — "Term A is exactly the kernel's available signal."** True for the NTK *at initialization* only. Term A $=\mathbb{E}[y\nabla_\theta f_0]$ is the correlation against the specific feature map $\nabla_\theta f_0$; killing it kills *that* kernel (this is literally Claim 5.2). It says nothing about an arbitrary kernel with a different feature map. You actually know this, because two sentences later you reach for the dimensional/margin-complexity machinery — which exists precisely because Term A $=0$ doesn't rule out general kernels. So the sentence as written contradicts your own plan. Fix: "Term A is the NTK-at-init's available signal."

**Conflation 2 — $M(D_X)$ is not label-free.** AGOP is $\mathbb{E}[\nabla_x h\,\nabla_x h^\top]$ for *some function $h$*, and you've annotated it "$D_X$ only." Those can't both hold. If $h=f_0$ it's label-free but won't generically surface a planted $v$ (it surfaces whatever the init put there). If $h$ is the target or the trained predictor, it's label-dependent. The multi-index fact you're invoking — AGOP eigenvectors span the relevant subspace — is a statement about the AGOP *of the target function*, which depends on the labels. You can't have an operator that is simultaneously a function of $D_X$ alone and a witness for the label-relevant direction, unless the task direction is encoded in $D_X$. Which is exactly the leaky-parities trick — so the resolution is: you must *engineer* $v$ to be both the label direction and a higher-moment marginal direction, the same coincidence Malach et al. arrange at first order. Once you say that, the "label-free" confusion dissolves, but you also see the construction burden: you're forcing two objects to coincide, not getting it for free.

**Conflation 3 — the "linear vs nonlinear" diagnosis is wrong for the part that matters.** "A kernel can only use a direction linearly" describes the NTK-at-init in their construction (where $\nabla_\theta f_0 = 2x$, so the kernel predictor is literally linear in $x$). But the lower bound you need is against *general* kernels, and general kernels have nonlinear feature maps — representing nonlinear functions is the entire point of a kernel. The reason a general kernel fails in leaky parities is **distribution-independence plus the exponential dimension/norm cost** of covering the family of possible targets, not linearity. So your conceptual punchline accidentally describes only the trivial half of the bound and mis-describes the half that's hard. The right contrast is *adaptive-to-$D_X$ vs not* — which, note, is exactly what RFM operationalizes.

**The technical crux you skip.** Malach et al.'s tractability comes entirely from the *single large step* trick. For one step to separate $v$, the gradient $\mathbb{E}[(f_0-y)\nabla_\theta f_0]$ must encode $v$. With $\nabla_\theta f_0$ linear in $x$ (as in their parity model), the step can only access *moments of $x$* up to low degree — and you've zeroed the first two. To reach a degree-3+ moment you need the gradient features $\nabla_\theta f_0(x)$ themselves to be cubic/quartic in $x$, i.e., a specific nonlinear-at-init construction. That's buildable (they already use bespoke activations and fixed subnetworks), but it's the actual work, and "GD with a nonlinear model" hand-waves past it. Without the single-step trick you lose their clean analysis.

**The NFA bridge is the weakest link.** $W^\top W \propto \text{AGOP}^\alpha$ is a *converged-training, two-layer-linear* result. Your construction is single-step and a bespoke fixed architecture. The regimes don't match, so invoking it is circular: you'd be assuming a convergence-time identity to analyze a one-step process. Worse, it links $\nabla_\theta f$ to $\nabla_x f$, which coincide only in degenerate constructions (theirs, because everything is linear in both). Drop the NFA as a load-bearing element; at most it's motivation in the intro.

**The novelty problem.** "GD recovers a direction hidden until high moments, on a mean-zero/identity-cov distribution" is the *information exponent / leap complexity* setup — Ben Arous–Gheissari–Jagannath, Abbe–Boix-Adserà–Misiakiewicz, plus the single-step results of Ba et al. and Damian et al. (the latter two are cited in paper 2). Your $v$-hidden-at-order-$k$ is essentially "information exponent $\geq 3$." If you pitch this as "GD sees higher-order structure," a reviewer will say it's known. The only defensible novel angle is the *kernel lower bound combined with the distribution-dependence framing* — so that has to be the explicit thesis, not a corollary.

**Aim the contribution correctly.** Paper 1's kernel bound is already maximal (exponential, against any poly-dim/poly-norm kernel) and is order-agnostic, so moving to higher moments does *not* strengthen the kernel side. What changes is the **GD side** — you'd be showing GD's marginal-exploitation reaches beyond first moments. So the result is about *GD's reach*, not about widening the GD-vs-kernel gap. And be clear-eyed that because AGOP-detectability of $v$ basically *guarantees* a data-dependent kernel (RFM-style) can detect it too, your separation is inherently against *distribution-independent* kernels — same category as their Section 5, not stronger. State that up front or you'll be accused of overselling.

**Verdict.** Worth pursuing only if you reframe it as: "a distribution-independent-kernel separation where the exploitable marginal structure is provably invisible to all moments below order $k$, with GD recovery via a single step requiring degree-$k$ gradient features at init." That's a real, narrow, defensible refinement. As currently framed — leaning on "Term A is the kernel signal," a label-free $M(D_X)$, a linear/nonlinear diagnosis, and the NFA bridge — it has four soft spots, three of which are factual rather than stylistic. Fix conflations 1–3, confront the information-exponent literature, and decide whether the kernel lower bound for a moment-blind orthogonal family of $v$'s actually goes through (that's a day of real work with the Kamath machinery, not a citation).
